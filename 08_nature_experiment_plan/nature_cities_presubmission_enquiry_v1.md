# Nature Cities Presubmission Enquiry v1
Date: 2026-07-13

## Proposed Title

Public InSAR observability gaps can undercount urban subsidence exposure

## 150-word Summary

Public InSAR products are increasingly used to monitor land subsidence in cities, but exposure accounting often treats missing coverage as neutral. We test whether this missingness behaves instead as structured censoring that hides the urban exposure most relevant to planning. In the Chao Phraya lead case, area-weighted accounting identifies 3.63 million hidden people and 422.78 km2 of hidden built-up land inside strongly deforming cells. Transport is retained as a companion sensitivity layer, not as the headline statistic. A cell-anchored synthesis and threshold/block-size robustness tests support the censoring direction, while Japan LiCSBAS and Iran public InSAR probes show that the question survives product-lineage changes. The claim is deliberately bounded: the paper does not offer a universal subsidence-risk map, but shows that public monitoring gaps can create city-scale monitoring debt and undercount exposure before urban risk is even assessed.

## Why Nature Cities

- The paper is about cities, infrastructure exposure, and policy-relevant monitoring gaps.
- The main message is urban exposure undercount, not InSAR method development.
- The claim is framed around people, built-up land, and urban decision-making.
- The transfer evidence is kept bounded and explicit.

## Distinction From Existing Nature Cities Subsidence Work

- Existing Nature Cities subsidence articles emphasize where subsidence threatens infrastructure and how much infrastructure is exposed.
- This paper asks a different question: whether open monitoring products systematically hide the urban exposure that should be counted in the first place.
- The novelty is observability bias as a source of exposure undercount, not another risk map.

## Novelty Bullets

1. Open-product observability is treated as structured censoring, not random missingness.
2. Exposure undercount is quantified with an urban lead case and area-weighted accounting.
3. People and built-up land are the manuscript-grade headline: 3.63 million hidden people and 422.78 km2 hidden built-up land in the lead case.
4. Cross-region transfer tests show that the issue survives product-lineage changes.
5. The paper provides a reproducible city-scale monitoring-debt argument with persistent code and data release.

## Suggested Abstract Hook

> Public monitoring products can make the most important urban exposure zones look safer than they are.
