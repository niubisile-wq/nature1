# Nature Cities Rejection Risk and Upgrade Memo v1
Date: 2026-07-14

## Purpose

This memo converts the current Nature Cities assessment into concrete rejection-risk controls. It is written for the stretch-target submission package, not as a general project note.

## Current probability readout

- Estimated direct acceptance probability after revision: `20-30%`.
- Estimated probability of passing editorial triage into external review: `40-50%`.
- Main reason this is not higher: the study is strongest as an observability-bias and exposure-accounting paper, but it still lacks a full external gold-standard closure and a city-level inequality layer.

## One-sentence argument

Open subsidence-monitoring products can systematically undercount urban exposure because the places that are least observable can also contain substantial people and built-up land inside strongly deforming zones.

## Five major rejection risks and required controls

| Rank | Likely rejection point | Why it matters for Nature Cities | Control added to package |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | The paper may look like an InSAR benchmark rather than urban science. | Nature Cities editors need people, built form, infrastructure, planning or governance relevance before technical product detail. | Title, abstract, introduction and cover letter now lead with urban exposure undercount and monitoring debt. |
| 2 | The novelty may look too close to existing subsidence-risk mapping papers. | The journal has already published subsidence-infrastructure risk work, so this paper must not read as another risk map. | Presubmission and cover letter explicitly distinguish `where subsidence threatens infrastructure` from `whether public monitoring hides the exposure to be counted`. |
| 3 | The headline evidence may feel qualitative. | Nature Cities articles often carry memorable numbers in the abstract or first result. | The Chao Phraya headline is now fixed as `3.63 million` hidden people and `422.78 km2` hidden built-up land, with transport kept as a sensitivity layer. |
| 4 | Transfer evidence may be read as overgeneralization. | Reviewers will object if Japan/Iran probes are presented as full validation. | Manuscript wording now labels transfer as product-lineage extension and keeps Europe-scale EGMS closure as the external upgrade path. |
| 5 | Missing vulnerability or inequality closure may weaken the city-science case. | Nature Cities often rewards distributional burden or equity evidence. | The paper now treats monitoring debt as the distributional claim and avoids promoting available vulnerability proxies beyond their evidence strength. |

## Upgrade actions already applied

1. Add quantitative Chao Phraya headline numbers to the abstract, lead-case result and conclusion.
2. Replace `benchmark`-first framing with `observability and exposure audit` framing in the abstract.
3. Add a clearer urban decision-making consequence: visible-only maps can remove under-observed zones from monitoring priority.
4. Strengthen the distinction from existing subsidence-risk papers in both presubmission and cover-letter material.
5. Make the limitation less damaging by naming it early: EGMS-enabled Europe-scale closure is an upgrade path, not a missing component of the current claim.

## Remaining risk after upgrade

- The paper still has a real desk-reject risk if the editor wants a larger multi-city direct exposure dataset rather than one lead case plus bounded transfer evidence.
- The strongest path is therefore a presubmission enquiry first, using the quantitative summary and novelty distinction.
- If the editor response is lukewarm, the fallback target should be a high-quality environmental hazards, remote-sensing, or urban analytics journal rather than forcing a full Nature Cities submission.

## Go/no-go judgment

The current package is defensible for a Nature Cities presubmission enquiry. It is not yet a low-risk direct full submission. The most efficient next move is to send the presubmission enquiry with the revised 150-word summary and the novelty distinction.
