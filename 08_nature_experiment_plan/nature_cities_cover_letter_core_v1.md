# Nature Cities Cover Letter Core v1
Date: 2026-07-13

## Core Positioning

This manuscript is not a new subsidence-mapping paper. It is an urban exposure-accounting paper.

The main claim is that public InSAR observability gaps can systematically undercount urban subsidence exposure, especially for people and built-up land in the most deforming areas. In the Chao Phraya lead case, the area-weighted closure identifies 3.63 million hidden people and 422.78 km2 of hidden built-up land inside strongly deforming cells.

## Why Nature Cities

- The problem is urban and policy-facing.
- The evidence is framed around cities, infrastructure exposure, and monitoring debt.
- The paper uses InSAR as the measurement substrate, not as the scientific endpoint.

## What is new

1. Observability is treated as structured censoring rather than random missingness.
2. The lead case is converted into an area-weighted urban exposure undercount.
3. The manuscript separates the headline exposure result from transport sensitivity and from regional transfer probes.
4. The release is reproducible, with versioned GitHub and Zenodo archiving.

## Why this is not the existing Nature Cities subsidence-infrastructure paper

- The existing paper maps where subsidence threatens infrastructure and how much infrastructure is exposed.
- This paper asks whether open monitoring products systematically hide the exposure that should be counted in the first place.
- The novelty is monitoring bias as a source of exposure undercount.

## Boundary handled upfront

- The transfer probes are used to show that the observability question survives product-lineage changes, not to claim universal validation.
- Transport infrastructure is retained as a companion sensitivity layer rather than mixed into the abstract headline.
- Europe-scale EGMS closure is presented as the next external validation route, not as a hidden prerequisite for the current claim.

## Safe closing line

We believe this work will be of interest to Nature Cities because it reframes public subsidence monitoring as a city-scale exposure accounting problem with direct implications for urban planning, risk prioritization, and monitoring policy.
